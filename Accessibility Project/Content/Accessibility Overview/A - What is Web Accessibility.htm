What is Web Accessibility?

Web accessibility, is the quality of a website, technical feature, or media modality being usable and accommodating to any person of any level of capability. When we create a website, an app, or even simple online document, we're not just publishing information; we're shaping an experience. And if you've ever tried to use a site with tiny text on a bright screen, a video without captions, or a form that doesn't work with your keyboard, you already have a glimpse of what accessibility is about.

As someone who creates, edits, designs, or manages content, you may have encountered guidelines or checklists about accessibility. I know that when I first explored the topic, it seemed highly technical and full of standards and acronyms. But at its core, web accessibility is about people. It ensures that someone who is blind and uses a screen reader, someone who is deaf and relies on captions, someone with limited mobility navigating by keyboard, or someone with cognitive differences processing information in unique ways can still fully participate online.

The Social Model

In common practices, there are different ways of defining web accessibility, as well as different ways of viewing disability. In Adobe’s Inclusive Design Workshop PDF, two models of viewing disabilities are presented: the social model and the medical model. The social model is defined as a person being “disabled by their environment”. In other words, it’s not simply a person’s blindness, deafness, mobility limitation, or cognitive difference that creates exclusion, it’s the barriers we build into the world around them. If a website cannot be navigated by keyboard, then the barrier is in the design. If a video has no captions, the barrier is in the production choice. The environment, not the person, is what disables.

When we talk about web accessibility, we’re not just discussing color contrast ratios or alt text. We’re also, whether we realize it or not, aligning ourselves with a broader philosophy about disability. And if you see your work as shaping environments that others must navigate, the social model offers a powerful reminder: we have the ability—and the responsibility—to design spaces that include rather than exclude.

The Medical Model

The other model described in Adobe’s Inclusive Design PDF is the medical model. This model is often quantified through metrics: “In this model, there are several thresholds

indicating “normal” sensory, physical, cognitive and emotional function. Should someone fall below one of these thresholds, the medical community considers them disabled”.

As someone creating digital content, this distinction matters. If we adopt a medical-model mindset, we may think, “How do we add accommodations later?” But if we embrace the social model, we ask from the start, “How might our design decisions disable someone, and how can we remove those barriers?” You can see how that shift changes everything: accessibility stops being an afterthought and becomes a core design principle.

The Core Principles of Accessibility

When we talk about the core principles of web accessibility, we’re really talking about a shared foundation for how you and I design digital experiences that work for everyone. If you’ve ever wondered, “What does accessibility actually require me to do?” the answer is often grounded in four key principles from the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), outlined in the Web Accessibility Initiative’s Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG).

These principles are commonly summarized as POUR: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust.

P: “Perceivable” means that users must be able to sense the content through at least one of their available senses. For example: If someone cannot see, text alternatives (like alt text for images) allow screen readers to convey meaning.

O: “Operable” means that interface components and navigation must be usable through different input methods. Not everyone uses a mouse. Some people rely on a keyboard, voice control, switch devices, or other assistive technologies.

U: “Understandable” refers both to the clarity of information and the predictability of the interface. If you are a writer or information designer, this principle speaks directly to your craft. Clear headings, plain language, consistent terminology, and logical structure all support comprehension.

R: “Robust means it should work reliably with a wide range of current and future technologies, including assistive technologies like screen readers.

Who Benefits From Accessibility?

Web accessibility benefits all of us, even if we don’t always notice it. If you are a person with a disability, whether related to vision, hearing, mobility, cognition, or neurodiversity, accessible design can mean the difference between independence and exclusion online. But the benefits don’t stop there. You and I rely on accessibility features when we watch captions in a noisy room, use voice commands while driving, or zoom in on small text on a mobile device. As creators, we also benefit: accessible websites reach wider audiences, improve usability, and create more inclusive digital spaces for everyone.
